A critical response to Stop Killing Games

Protect players, without breaking game development.

Players deserve honest storefronts, meaningful shutdown notice, fair refunds, and better preservation efforts. But broad legal mandates that try to force every online game to remain playable after shutdown risk creating impossible obligations for studios, especially smaller teams building live or experimental games.

Our position in short
Yes to stronger consumer protections Clear purchase labels, server dependency warnings, shutdown notice, and appropriate remedies.
No to vague one-size-fits-all mandates Not every game can safely or legally be converted into an offline or community-run product.
Technical reality matters Live service infrastructure, anti-cheat systems, moderation, licensing, and cloud architecture create real constraints.
Preservation should still be encouraged But through targeted incentives, transparent policies, and practical case-by-case solutions.
The central point

The frustration is real. The proposed fix is too broad.

The campaign begins with a valid complaint: people can lose access to games they paid for. The problem is that it often treats every shutdown as if it were a simple, reversible decision, when many games are deeply tied to servers, live operations, third-party tools, licensing agreements, and security systems that cannot be cleanly separated at the end of a product’s life.

“A good preservation policy should make the market more honest — not make every connected game a permanent legal liability.”

That is the core disagreement. Preservation is worth pursuing. Blanket obligations are not.

It creates legal ambiguity

Terms like “playable after shutdown” or “reasonable end-of-life support” sound intuitive, but can become unpredictable in practice. Developers may not know what regulators or courts would expect years later.

Not all games are just software products

Some are closer to services, social platforms, economies, or live moderated spaces. Treating them all like static boxed software ignores how modern online games actually function.

Retrofitting offline support can mean rebuilding the game

Server authority, matchmaking, inventory, anti-cheat, persistence, moderation, and content delivery are often foundational, not optional layers.

The burden falls hardest on smaller teams

Large publishers may absorb compliance costs. Smaller studios may simply stop making connected games, avoid live features, or never experiment at all.

Why oppose the campaign?

Because the side effects could be worse than supporters expect.

A law designed to stop games from disappearing could unintentionally reduce innovation, increase compliance costs, complicate preservation, and reinforce the advantage of the largest publishers.

1. It could chill experimentation

If every online feature comes with unclear future obligations, studios will design more conservatively. That means fewer weird multiplayer concepts, fewer live prototypes, and fewer risky ideas.

2. It ignores security reality

Server binaries, backend logic, anti-cheat systems, moderation tools, and account infrastructure can expose vulnerabilities if released without strict control. What looks like preservation from one angle may look like a security incident from another.

3. It underestimates licensing constraints

Music, sports leagues, cars, brands, middleware, voice rights, cloud vendors, and regional publishing agreements can all restrict what may remain distributed or transferred after shutdown.

4. It can raise costs for everyone

More legal review, longer maintenance planning, documentation requirements, compliance overhead, and support obligations do not vanish. They are usually paid for through higher prices, smaller scope, or reduced support for new projects.

5. It may still fail to preserve what matters

Preserving a game is not always the same as preserving a community, a seasonal meta, a live economy, or a specific social moment. Law alone cannot reconstruct those things after a service ends.

6. It can unintentionally favor the biggest companies

Heavy obligations often become easier for large publishers to absorb than for indies. That is how “consumer friendly” legislation can accidentally lock smaller creators out of the market.

A better alternative

Be pro-player without mandating the impossible.

Opposing Stop Killing Games does not mean defending bad business behavior. It means supporting a more precise framework that improves customer fairness without pretending every game can be preserved the same way.

Clear purchase labels

Storefronts should distinguish between durable offline products, online-dependent services, subscriptions, rentals, and revocable licenses. Customers should know exactly what they are buying.

Visible server dependency disclosures

If core progression, access, or basic gameplay depends on external servers, that should be plainly stated before checkout — not buried in legal terms.

Minimum shutdown notice

Publishers should provide meaningful advance notice when they intend to retire a paid online game or remove major functionality.

Fair remedies

If access ends unusually soon after purchase, regulators can require refunds, credits, or other compensation without forcing permanent technical support.

Voluntary preservation incentives

Encourage offline modes, archival builds, private server handoff plans, preservation partnerships, or museum access where feasible, safe, and legal.

Security and privacy carve-outs

Any framework must explicitly protect player data, anti-cheat methods, internal infrastructure, and security-sensitive systems.

Safe harbors for smaller teams

Indies, limited-run projects, free tests, and low-revenue live games should not be burdened with requirements designed around large publishers.

Frequently asked questions

Full FAQ

This section is written for people who support the campaign, are unsure about it, or simply want the strongest arguments from the opposing side.

Are you against game preservation? +

No. Preservation is valuable. The disagreement is about method. We support preservation where possible, but reject broad legal mandates that assume every online game can be made playable forever.

A good preservation strategy should be practical, proportionate, and sensitive to licensing, infrastructure, privacy, security, and team size.

Why oppose Stop Killing Games specifically? +

Because its legislative direction is too broad. It begins from a real consumer problem, but can easily turn into vague obligations that force developers to provide technical outcomes they may not be able to deliver safely, cheaply, or legally.

Why not just require an offline mode? +

Because many online games do not contain a simple hidden offline version. Their core systems may rely on server authority, matchmaking, economies, progression databases, anti-cheat, live moderation, or third-party services.

In those cases, “just add offline mode” can mean extensive redevelopment.

Why not release the server software to the community? +

Sometimes that can work. Sometimes it cannot. Server code may include security-sensitive systems, licensed middleware, anti-cheat components, internal tooling, or integrations that cannot be redistributed.

Community servers are a useful option in some cases, not a universal obligation.

But I paid for the game. Shouldn’t I keep it? +

You should absolutely know what you are paying for. The strongest consumer protection is honest disclosure at the moment of sale.

If a product is temporary access to an online service, that should be obvious. If it is sold like a durable standalone product, then buyers have a stronger claim to expect lasting access.

So what should stores be required to show? +

At minimum: whether the game requires external servers, whether offline play exists, what functionality may disappear, and whether the publisher has a shutdown or end-of-life policy.

Are you just defending publishers? +

No. Publishers should not be allowed to market products deceptively, hide server dependency, or sell access right before shutdown without fair remedies.

We are defending better law, not bad conduct.

Why do security concerns matter so much? +

Because old infrastructure can expose player data, account systems, exploit paths, anti-cheat logic, and backend vulnerabilities. Releasing or maintaining those systems after shutdown is not always safe.

Would this hurt indie developers? +

Potentially, yes. Large publishers may afford compliance, legal review, archival planning, and extended maintenance obligations. Smaller studios usually cannot.

That means the burden lands hardest on the people least able to absorb it.

What about multiplayer-only or MMO games? +

Those are among the hardest cases. They are often not just software, but economies, communities, moderation systems, competitive ecosystems, and continuously updated services.

Preserving them may require more than just keeping a binary executable alive.

What about licensed content like music, cars, sports, or brands? +

Those licenses often expire. A developer may not legally be allowed to continue distributing certain assets, names, voices, logos, or branded content after a given period.

What is the fairest consumer remedy when a game shuts down? +

Usually some combination of advance notice, refunds, store credit, migration support, or partial compensation — especially if the paid product had a very short life after sale.

What is your final position? +

We oppose the broad legislative direction of Stop Killing Games. We support clearer consumer rights, better disclosure, fairer shutdown practices, and stronger preservation efforts where feasible.

We reject one-size-fits-all mandates that could harm creators, reduce experimentation, and create obligations that many studios cannot realistically meet.

Better rules. Better honesty. Better preservation.

Support players with clarity, notice, and fair remedies — without turning every online game into an indefinite legal burden.
